How to Perform a FAST Examination

FAST examination overview

FAST views

The FAST protocol consists of four standard views:

View Plane Probe position Probe marker Main structures visualised
Right Upper Quadrant (RUQ) Coronal (frontal) Right posterior axillary line, through the lower intercostal spaces Towards the patient’s head Liver, right kidney, hepatorenal recess (Morison pouch), diaphragm, pleural space, spine
Left Upper Quadrant (LUQ) Coronal (frontal) Left posterior axillary line, through the lower intercostal spaces Towards the patient’s head Spleen, left kidney, splenorenal recess, diaphragm, pleural space, spine
Suprapubic Transverse Midline, immediately above the pubic symphysis Towards the patient’s right Bladder, rectovesical/rectouterine pouch, uterus or prostate
Subxiphoid Transverse Immediately inferior to the xiphoid process, directed towards the left shoulder Towards the patient’s right Liver, pericardium, four cardiac chambers

Tip

The suprapubic examination should ideally include both transverse and sagittal views, as this improves the sensitivity for detecting small volumes of pelvic free fluid.

Which probe to choose?

The FAST exam visualises deep structures and requires a low frequency probe. Literature is heterogeneous for choice between abdominal and sectorial probes, and both may be used in practice.

Right Upper Quadrant (Morison pouch)

The RUQ view evaluates the hepatorenal recess (Morison pouch) and the right paracolic gutter.

Image Acquisition

Place the probe on the right posterior axillary line, through the lowest intercostal spaces, with the probe marker pointing towards the patient’s head.

A satisfactory image should simultaneously include:

  • liver
  • right kidney
  • hepatorenal interface (Morison pouch)
  • diaphragm
  • spine

Troubleshooting

  • If the probe is too superior, aerated lung obscures the image.
  • If the probe is too anterior, bowel gas limits visualization.
  • Slide posteriorly until both the liver and kidney appear in the same image.
  • Ask the patient to take a deep inspiration, if possible, to bring the liver below the rib cage.

Interpretation

Look for anechoic (black) free fluid within:

  • the hepatorenal recess (Morison pouch)
  • the inferior tip of Morison pouch
  • the right paracolic gutter

Left Upper Quadrant (splenorenal recess)

The LUQ view evaluates the splenorenal recess and the left paracolic gutter

Compared with the RUQ, this view is usually more technically challenging because the spleen is smaller and positioned more posteriorly and superiorly.

Image Acquisition

Place the probe on the left posterior axillary line through the lowest intercostal spaces with the probe marker directed towards the patient’s head.

A satisfactory image should include:

  • spleen
  • left kidney
  • splenorenal recess
  • diaphragm
  • spine extending above the diaphragm

Troubleshooting

  • Position the probe slightly more posterior and cephalad than on the right side.
  • Scan through the intercostal spaces.
  • Ask the patient to inhale deeply whenever possible.

Interpretation

Search for anechoic free fluid in:

  • the splenorenal recess
  • around the inferior pole of the spleen
  • the left paracolic gutter

Suprapubic views (Douglas pouch)

The pelvic view evaluates the Douglas pouch. Both transverse and sagittal views should ideally be obtained.

Transverse view

Image Acquisition

Place the probe immediately above the pubic symphysis with the probe marker pointing towards the patient’s right.

The bladder should be used as an acoustic window.

The examination should visualise:

  • Male: bladder, rectovesical pouch (Douglas), rectum
  • Female: bladder, uterus, rectouterine pouch (Douglas), rectum

Sweep the probe from the bladder dome towards its base using a short-axis sliding or tilting motion.

Troubleshooting

  • Direct the probe slightly posteriorly if the bladder is difficult to visualise.
  • A partially filled bladder is usually sufficient.
  • Gas within the rectum may obscure posterior structures.
  • The seminal vesicles may resemble fluid, but unlike free fluid they have smooth, symmetric margins and do not conform to surrounding anatomy.

Interpretation

Look for free fluid:

  • between the bladder and rectum in males
  • between the uterus and rectum in females

Sagittal view

Rotate the probe 90° clockwise so that the probe marker points towards the patient’s head.

Sweep from one side of the bladder to the other while maintaining the bladder in the centre of the image.

The sagittal view improves the detection of small fluid collections and better demonstrates the uterus and rectouterine pouch.

Subxiphoid cardiac view

The subxiphoid view evaluates the pericardial space for hemopericardium.

Image Acquisition

Place the probe immediately inferior to the xiphoid process with the probe marker directed towards the patient’s right. Aim the ultrasound beam towards the patient’s left shoulder.

Use the liver as an acoustic window.

The image should include:

  • liver
  • right ventricle
  • left ventricle
  • right atrium
  • left atrium
  • pericardium

Note that right cavities are closer from the probe than left cavities.

If gastric gas limits the view, apply gentle pressure towards the spine rather than towards the xiphoid process. Pressure on the xyphoid process is painful and must be avoided.

Troubleshooting

  • Increase posterior angulation towards the heart.
  • Ask the patient to take a deep inspiration if clinically feasible.
  • Slide slightly to the patient’s right to maximise the liver acoustic window, if spine trauma is not suspected.

Interpretation

Look for anechoic fluid surrounding the heart.

The presence of a pericardial effusion does not by itself indicate cardiac tamponade and requires additional findings, such as right-sided chamber collapse and clinical features.

In an unstable trauma patient, a large pericardial effusion (> 1 cm) without other obvious cause of shock strongly suggests tamponade and should prompt immediate consideration of pericardial drainage.
