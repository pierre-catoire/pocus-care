What is an ultrasound?

Nature of ultrasound waves

An ultrasound is a mechanical wave that propagates through matter as successive variations in pressure. Unlike electromagnetic waves such as light or X-rays, ultrasound cannot travel through a vacuum and requires a physical medium.

In medical imaging, ultrasound waves propagate through biological tissues by causing microscopic cycles of compression and rarefaction. Although these pressure variations are extremely small, they can travel through tissues and carry information back to the ultrasound probe.

An ultrasound wave is characterized by several properties, among which frequency, amplitude and wavelength are the most important.

Frequency corresponds to the number of oscillations occurring each second and is expressed in hertz (Hz). Diagnostic ultrasound typically uses frequencies between 2 and 15 MHz.

Amplitude corresponds to the magnitude of the pressure variation generated by the wave.

Wavelength is the distance travelled during one oscillation cycle. It is related to frequency by:

\[ \lambda = \frac{c}{f} \]

where:

  • \(\lambda\) = wavelength
  • \(c\) = speed of sound in the medium
  • \(f\) = frequency
TipKey Definitions
  • Frequency: number of oscillations per second.
  • Amplitude: magnitude of pressure variation.
  • Wavelength: distance covered during one oscillation cycle.
  • Ultrasound: sound waves with frequencies above 20 kHz. Medical ultrasound typically uses frequencies between 2 and 15 MHz.
NoteAdvanced: Ultrasound Energy

The acoustic energy of an ultrasound wave (measured in Joules, \(J\)) is the mechanical energy transported through a medium by successive compressions and rarefactions.

In medical ultrasound, two related quantities are particularly useful:

  • Power (Watts, \(W\)): the amount of acoustic energy emitted per unit time.
  • Intensity (\(W/m^2\)): the acoustic power transmitted per unit area.

In practice, the user of an ultrasound device can influence several parameters related to acoustic energy:

  • Acoustic power (output power): increasing the acoustic power increases the amount of energy emitted per unit time.
  • Pulse duration: longer pulses contain more energy and therefore deliver more energy during each emission.
  • Pulse repetition frequency (PRF): increasing the PRF increases the number of emitted pulses per second and therefore increases the average power delivered to tissues.

Note that the ultrasound frequency does not directly determine the emitted energy. However, higher-frequency waves are attenuated more rapidly in tissues and therefore deposit their energy over a shorter distance.

Finally, gain is a signal amplification parameter applied to received echoes. It does not modify the ultrasound waves emitted by the probe and therefore has no effect on acoustic energy.

Frequency, Penetration and Resolution

The frequency affects both resolution and penetration:

  • Low-frequency waves can travel deeper through tissues before becoming attenuated. They therefore provide better penetration and are used to image deep structures such as the heart or abdominal organs.

  • High-frequency waves are attenuated more rapidly. Their penetration is therefore limited. However, because their wavelength is shorter, they can distinguish smaller structures and provide superior spatial resolution.

Low Frequency High Frequency
Better penetration Better resolution
Lower resolution Lower penetration
Use for superficial exam (skin, superficial vessels …) Use for deep exam (abdomen, heart, brain …)
TipA useful analogy

A useful analogy is the music heard from a distant party. Deep bass sounds remain audible from far away, whereas high-pitched sounds disappear more quickly. Similarly, low-frequency ultrasound travels deeper into tissues, whereas high-frequency ultrasound provides finer anatomical detail.

Emission, Propagation, Absorption and Scattering

Emission and the piezo-electric effect

Ultrasound waves are generated by the probe.

The probe contains multiple piezoelectric crystals capable of converting electrical energy into mechanical vibrations and mechanical vibrations into electrical signals. This phenomenon is known as the piezoelectric effect. The same crystals therefore act both as emitters and receivers.

Propagation Through Biological Tissues

Once emitted, ultrasound waves propagate through tissues.

For imaging purposes, ultrasound systems assume an average propagation speed of:

\[ c = 1540\ \text{m/s} \]

in soft tissues.

Attenuation, Absorption and Scattering

As the wave propagates, part of its energy is progressively lost. This phenomenon is called attenuation.

Attenuation results mainly from:

  • Absorption: Part of the mechanical energy is converted into heat.
  • Scattering: Small structures redirect ultrasound energy in multiple directions.

Reflection and transmission

Each medium (water, fat, bone, gas …) has its own acoustic impedance (noted \(Z\)), which describes how easily the ultrasound propagates.

Acoustic impedances of biological tissues, in MRayl (\(10^6 \cdot kg \cdot m^{-2} \cdot s^{-1}\))
Water Fat Bone Gas
1.48 1.33 6-8 0.0004

As long as the medium is homogeneous, the impedance is constant and no reflection occurs. However, when the wave transitions from a medium to another (the surface between them is called the interface), the ultrasound wave separates between transmitted and reflected ultrasounds:

  • the transmitted ultrasound continues towards the new medium
  • the reflected ultrasound bounces back in the opposite direction

Note that because the energy is conserved, the sum of the energies of the transmitted and reflected ultrasound, and the energy between the ultrasound hitting the interface (called the “incident” ultrasound) is constant:

  • strong reflection implies weak transmission;
  • weak reflection implies strong transmission.

Differences in acoustic impedance determine the intensity of the reflection and transmission:

  • an interface between two media with large difference of impedance is highly reflective
  • an interface between two media with small difference of impedance is weakly reflective
ImportantCommon Misconception

Many learners believe that bone is the strongest reflector of ultrasound.

Bone reflects a large proportion of ultrasound energy, but gas reflects even more. Actually, ultrasound is possible through bones (for example, transcranial doppler), but impossible through gas.

From an ultrasound perspective, gas behaves like a mirror. No structure can be seen if gas is present between the probe and the structure. This is why:

  • ultrasound gel is needed: without gel, a thin layer of air remains trapped between the probe and the skin.
  • ultrasound can see only the superficial layers of the bowel and the pleura
  • if pneumothorax is present, only the parietal pleura is visible, the visceral pleura is hidden by the gas layer of the pneumothorax
WarningKey points
  • An ultrasound is a mechanical wave, not an electromagnetic wave
  • it is characterised by its frequency and amplitude
  • the lower frequencies penetrate better but have poorer resolution
  • when hitting an interface, a part of the ultrasound is reflected and the remaining part is transmitted to the new medium
  • gas is highly reflective in human tissues
