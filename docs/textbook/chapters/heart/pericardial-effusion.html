Pericardial Effusion

Author

Pierre Catoire

Identifying a Pericardial Effusion

Typical characteristics of pericardial effusion

Pericardial effusion manifests as an anechoic collection peripheral to the heart. It can be:

  • Circumferential
  • or localised

It can be observed in any heart view.

  • Parasternal Long Axis
  • Parasternal Short Axis
  • Apical Four Chambers
  • Subcostal

Differential diagnosis

Epicardial fat pad

Small effusions may be difficult to identify and distinguish from epicardial fat pad.

Differential diagnosis between pericardial effusion and epicardial fat pad.
Feature Pericardial effusion Epicardial fat pad
Echogenicity Anechoic or nearly anechoic More echogenic, heterogeneous, often with internal speckes
Movement Fluid remains stationary while the heart moves with it Moves synchronously with the myocardium
Typical location Circumferential or dependent; often posterior when small Most commonly anterior, especially over the right ventricle
Shape Smooth fluid layer conforming to the pericardial space Irregular or lobulated appearance
Dynamic appearance Maintains an echo-gree space throughout the cardiac cycle Thickness and contour change as the myocardium contracts

Left pleural effusion

Left pleural effusion can mimick a pericardial effusion. The best distinctive feature is the presence of fluid between the left atrium and the descending aorta, confirming the pericardial effusion. In pleural effusions, the fluid stays lateral to the descending aorta.

Importance and hemodynamic consequences

Once a pericardial effusion has been identified, the assessment aims at:

  • estimating the importance of the effusion
  • evaluating the consequences on cardiac function

Importance of the effusion

The importance of the effusion is measured in end of diastole. ESC 2025 guidelines classify pericardial effusion as:

Pericardial effusion size classification. From (1).
Effusion size Classification
< 10 mm Mild
10-20 mm Moderate
> 20 mm Large

Importantly, the importance of the effusion is poorly related to the consequences on the heart: although effusions inferior to 10 mm are not considered as significant, mild or moderate effusions can cause tamponade whereas important effusions can be asymptomatic.

Important

Volume of the effusion is poorly correlated with severity.

Cardiac consequences

The following signs suggest significant hemodynamic compromise, characteristic of tamponade:

  • Right Atrium Diastolic Collapse (RADC)
  • early Right Ventricular Diastolic Collapse (RVDC)
  • IVC dilatation
