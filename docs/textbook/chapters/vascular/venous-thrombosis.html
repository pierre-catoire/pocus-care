Venous Thrombosis

Strong evidence supports positive predictive value of POCUS diagnosis of deep vein thrombosis, with a pooled sensitivity estimated at 95%, and specificity at 96% (1).

Current international guidelines (2) recommend compression ultrasonography as the first-line imaging test for suspected proximal DVT. Demonstration of a non-compressible proximal vein is considered diagnostic of DVT. Multiple studies have shown that clinician-performed compression POCUS achieves diagnostic accuracy comparable to comprehensive duplex ultrasound when performed by appropriately trained operators, supporting its use for bedside diagnosis in emergency and critical care settings (3) (4) (1).

A positive compression POCUS demonstrating loss of complete venous compressibility is highly specific for proximal DVT and, when performed by a trained operator in an appropriate clinical setting, can be considered sufficient to rule in DVT.

  • doppler flow has not shown improvement in diagnosis accuracy (5)

Areas of assessment

Three protocols are usually described:

“4-point” examination protocol

The “4-point” protocol evaluates bilaterally the compressibility of:

  • the common femoral vein, at Scarpa’s triangle, with the saphenofemoral jonction

  • the popliteal vein, with the popliteal trifurcation

This protocol has been widely disseminated, but is nowadays oudated as it may miss isolated femoral vein thromboses between popliteal and femoral exam sites.

“6-point” examination protocol

This variant of the 4-point examination protocol includes exam of the femoral vein on his proximal and median course, as long as visualisable. It reduces the risk of false negatives while still rapid an efficient.

Whole leg protocol

The whole leg protocol includes assessment of:

  • External iliac vein (when feasible)

  • Femoral vein along its entire course

  • Popliteal vein

  • Calf veins: posterior tibial, peroneal , gastrocnemius, soleal muscular veins

This protocol requires significant expertise and time, and is not suitable for POCUS exam.

POCUS venous exam in practice

In practice, a systematic compression of the common femoral, femoral, and popliteal veins, with compression every 1–2 cm along the course of the proximal veins is the most suitable for most cases.
